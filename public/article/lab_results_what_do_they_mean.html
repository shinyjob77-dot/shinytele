Lab Results: What Do They Mean?

Learn why lab results can be confusing and how telehealth review may help you understand the next steps.

Getting lab results can sometimes feel confusing or stressful, especially when you see numbers marked "high," "low," or "abnormal." Many patients receive results before they have a chance to speak with a healthcare provider, which can lead to worry, internet searching, and uncertainty about what the numbers actually mean.

Lab results are one part of your overall health picture. A result that is slightly outside the normal range does not always mean something serious, and a result in the normal range does not always explain all symptoms. Lab results should be reviewed along with your symptoms, medical history, medications, age, health conditions, and reason the test was ordered.

Common Lab Tests

Common lab tests may include blood count, kidney function, liver enzymes, electrolytes, cholesterol levels, thyroid tests, blood sugar, A1C, urine testing, vitamin levels, and infection-related testing. Each test gives different information. Some labs help screen for health problems, some monitor chronic conditions, and others help guide medication safety or treatment decisions.

Normal Ranges and Abnormal Results

It is important to understand that "normal range" can vary depending on the lab, the test, and the person. Sometimes a mild abnormal result may only need repeat testing or monitoring. Other results may require lifestyle changes, medication review, additional testing, or referral to a specialist.

When Telehealth Can Help

Telehealth can be helpful for reviewing many lab results when the results are available and your symptoms are stable. During a virtual visit, your provider can explain what the results may mean, review trends over time, discuss symptoms, review medications, and help plan next steps. This may include lifestyle guidance, medication adjustment, follow-up labs, or referral when appropriate.

When to Seek Urgent Care

Some lab results or symptoms should not wait for a routine telehealth visit. Seek urgent or emergency care if you have chest pain, severe shortness of breath, fainting, confusion, severe weakness, severe abdominal pain, signs of stroke, severe dehydration, uncontrolled bleeding, or symptoms that are worsening quickly.

Lab results should not be interpreted based on one number alone. Patterns, trends, and the reason for testing matter. If you are unsure about your results, it is better to ask questions than to guess or worry alone.
