Type 2 Diabetes and When Telehealth Can Help

Type 2 diabetes is a long-term condition where blood sugar levels are higher than they should be because the body does not use insulin well or does not make enough insulin over time. With the right care plan, monitoring, medication support, and lifestyle changes, many people can manage Type 2 diabetes and reduce complications.

Type 2 diabetes symptoms can develop slowly. Some people do not notice symptoms at first. Others may have increased thirst, frequent urination, fatigue, blurry vision, increased hunger, slow-healing sores, frequent infections, numbness or tingling in the feet, or unexplained weight changes.

Diabetes care is not only about one number. It includes blood sugar readings, A1C results, blood pressure, cholesterol, kidney health, eye care, foot care, nutrition, activity, medications, and prevention of long-term complications.

When Telehealth May Help

Telehealth can help with many routine Type 2 diabetes follow-up needs, especially when symptoms are stable. A telehealth visit may include blood sugar log review, A1C discussion, medication review, refill planning, side effect review, lifestyle counseling, nutrition discussion, and lab follow-up.

Your provider may ask about fasting blood sugar, readings after meals, low blood sugar episodes, missed doses, diet changes, weight changes, exercise, symptoms, home blood pressure, kidney labs, cholesterol, foot symptoms, eye exam status, and medication cost or access.

Home Blood Sugar Monitoring

Some people with Type 2 diabetes need to check blood sugar regularly, especially if they take insulin, have low blood sugar episodes, or are making medication changes. Others may check less often depending on their treatment plan.

A glucose meter or continuous glucose monitor can provide helpful information, but the results need context. Write down the time of the reading, whether it was before or after meals, symptoms, medications, and any unusual food, activity, or illness.

Medication Safety and Labs

Diabetes medications can be very helpful, but they require careful review. Some medications can cause low blood sugar, stomach symptoms, dehydration, urinary or genital infections, kidney concerns, or other side effects. Medication choices depend on your health history, kidney function, heart health, weight goals, cost, and safety.

Labs are an important part of diabetes care. Your provider may discuss A1C, kidney function, urine protein testing, cholesterol, liver function, and other labs depending on your medications and overall health.

When Telehealth Is Not Enough

Telehealth cannot check your feet in detail, perform a full physical exam, draw blood during the visit, treat severe dehydration, or manage a diabetes emergency. Some symptoms need urgent in-person care.

Seek urgent care or emergency care if you have very high blood sugar with vomiting, severe weakness, confusion, fruity breath, deep or rapid breathing, severe dehydration, chest pain, fainting, severe infection, a foot wound that is worsening, or symptoms of very low blood sugar that do not improve quickly with treatment.

Call 911 for severe confusion, loss of consciousness, seizure, chest pain, severe shortness of breath, stroke symptoms, or any life-threatening symptom.
