Mild Comedonal Acne Vulgaris
ICD: L70.0, L70.8
 L70.0 Acne vulgaris
L70.8 Other acne		 Open and closed comedones, few inflammatory papules, often forehead/T-zone. Blackheads/whiteheads; minimal redness; no painful nodules. Topical retinoid +/- benzoyl peroxide; gentle cleanser and non-comedogenic moisturizer.
  • Adapalene 0.1% gel nightly
  • Tretinoin 0.025-0.05% cream nightly
  • Benzoyl peroxide 2.5-5% daily
 Generics usually preferred. Brand retinoids may need failure of OTC adapalene or generic tretinoin. Adapalene OTC about $12-25/month; tretinoin generic about $20-80/month cash. Excellent
Works well with clear photos.		 No improvement after 8-12 weeks; worsening irritation; uncertain diagnosis.
Moderate Inflammatory Acne
ICD: L70.0
 L70.0 Acne vulgaris Papules, pustules, inflammatory lesions on face, chest, or back. Red inflamed lesions; may have comedones plus pustules. Topical retinoid + benzoyl peroxide +/- topical antibiotic.
  • Benzoyl peroxide wash AM
  • Clindamycin 1% gel/solution AM
  • Tretinoin or adapalene PM
 Combination products may be denied unless separate generics tried first. Document duration and OTC failures. Clindamycin topical about $15-40/month; combination products about $50-200 cash. Excellent Scarring, painful lesions, rapid worsening, treatment failure.
Moderate-Severe Inflammatory Acne
ICD: L70.0
 L70.0 Acne vulgaris Widespread papules/pustules, back/chest involvement, higher scarring risk. More extensive inflammation; may be painful and persistent. Add short course oral antibiotic while continuing topical therapy.
  • Doxycycline 100 mg daily to BID x 8-12 weeks
  • Minocycline 50-100 mg BID
  • Continue benzoyl peroxide to reduce resistance
 Oral doxycycline/minocycline generally covered. Document moderate/severe inflammatory acne and prior topical therapy. Doxycycline often about $10-40/month generic cash. Good
Requires counseling on photosensitivity and pregnancy avoidance.		 Scarring, severe pain, suspected infection, no response after 12 weeks.
Hormonal Acne / Female Pattern Acne
ICD: L70.0; E28.2 if PCOS
 L70.0 Acne vulgaris
E28.2 PCOS, if associated		 Jawline/chin acne, adult female, cyclic flares around menses. May be associated with irregular periods, hirsutism, or PCOS history. Topical acne therapy plus hormonal therapy when appropriate.
  • Spironolactone 25-100 mg daily
  • Combined oral contraceptive if appropriate
  • Topical retinoid + benzoyl peroxide
 Spironolactone usually covered; document hormonal distribution and failed topical therapy. OCP coverage varies. Spironolactone about $5-20/month generic cash. Good
Screen pregnancy status and contraindications.		 Irregular menses, hirsutism, infertility concerns, rapid virilization - consider endocrine/gynecology referral.
Nodulocystic / Severe Acne
ICD: L70.0, L70.8
 L70.0 Acne vulgaris
L70.8 Other acne		 Deep painful nodules/cysts, scarring, significant psychosocial distress. Deep lesions rather than superficial pustules; high scar risk. Early dermatology referral; consider isotretinoin evaluation.
  • Isotretinoin 0.5-1 mg/kg/day under appropriate monitoring
  • Interim doxycycline/topicals may be used if appropriate
 Often requires PA, failed topical + oral therapy documentation, pregnancy prevention requirements, labs, and specialist involvement. Generic isotretinoin often about $150-400/month cash; plan-specific copays vary. Limited
Monitoring and iPLEDGE-related requirements apply.		 Refer
Severe scarring, cysts, depression related to acne, isotretinoin candidate.
Rosacea
ICD: L71.9
 L71.9 Rosacea, unspecified Central facial redness, flushing, papules/pustules on cheeks/nose. No comedones; triggers include heat, alcohol, spicy foods, sun. Trigger avoidance, gentle skin care, topical anti-inflammatory therapy.
  • Metronidazole gel/cream
  • Azelaic acid 15-20%
  • Doxycycline for inflammatory papules if needed
 Metronidazole usually covered. Brand topical ivermectin/azelaic acid may require PA or step therapy. Metronidazole about $20-100/month; azelaic acid varies widely. Good Eye pain, vision changes, severe ocular symptoms - urgent eye/derm evaluation.
Bacterial Folliculitis
ICD: L73.9, L73.8
 L73.9 Follicular disorder, unspecified
L73.8 Other follicular disorders		 Follicle-centered pustules, often after shaving, sweating, occlusion. Lesions centered on hair follicles; may be tender. Antibacterial wash, topical antibiotic; oral antibiotic if extensive.
  • Benzoyl peroxide wash
  • Topical clindamycin
  • Oral cephalexin/doxycycline depending on presentation
 Most generics covered. Culture may help recurrent or resistant cases. Usually low cost; common generics often $10-40. Good Fever, spreading redness, abscess, cellulitis, immunocompromised patient.
Malassezia Folliculitis "Fungal Acne"
ICD: B36.0, L73.9
 B36.0 Other superficial mycoses
L73.9 Folliculitis		 Monomorphic itchy papules/pustules, often chest/back/forehead. Often itchy; worsens with sweat/heat; lacks typical comedones. Topical antifungal therapy and reduce occlusive products.
  • Ketoconazole 2% shampoo as wash
  • Ketoconazole cream
  • Oral fluconazole only when clinically appropriate
 Ketoconazole usually covered; oral antifungals require appropriate diagnosis and interaction review. Ketoconazole often $10-30 cash. Good Persistent/recurrent rash, unclear diagnosis, widespread disease.
Perioral Dermatitis
ICD: L71.0
 L71.0 Perioral dermatitis Papules around mouth, nose, or eyes; burning/tightness possible. Spares vermillion border; often worsened by topical steroids. Stop topical steroids; gentle skin care; topical or oral therapy.
  • Metronidazole topical
  • Erythromycin topical
  • Doxycycline for moderate/severe cases
 Usually covered with generic medications. Low to moderate cost depending on medication. Good Eye involvement, severe spread, steroid withdrawal flare needing specialist care.
Steroid Acne
ICD: L70.8
 L70.8 Other acne Sudden uniform papules after systemic or topical steroid exposure. Monomorphic eruption; temporal relationship to steroid use. Discontinue/reduce steroid if medically appropriate; standard acne treatment.
  • Benzoyl peroxide wash
  • Topical retinoid
  • Consider oral antibiotic if inflammatory
 No special PA; document medication trigger. Variable, usually low with generics. Good Do not abruptly stop required systemic steroids without managing provider involvement.
Medication-Induced Acneiform Eruption
ICD: L27.0, L70.8
 L27.0 Generalized skin eruption due to drugs
L70.8 Other acne		 New acneiform eruption after lithium, testosterone, steroids, anticonvulsants, EGFR inhibitors, etc. Timing after medication start; often monomorphic. Review medication list; coordinate with prescriber; treat symptoms.
  • Topical benzoyl peroxide/retinoid
  • Doxycycline if inflammatory and appropriate
  • Avoid stopping essential medications without coordination
 Coverage depends on selected acne medication; document suspected drug trigger. Variable. Good Severe drug rash, mucosal involvement, systemic symptoms - urgent evaluation.
Hidradenitis Suppurativa
ICD: L73.2
 L73.2 Hidradenitis suppurativa Recurrent painful nodules/abscesses in axilla, groin, under breasts, buttocks. Sinus tracts, scarring, recurrent drainage; not typical facial acne. Topical clindamycin for mild; oral antibiotics; lifestyle counseling; dermatology for moderate/severe.
  • Clindamycin 1% topical BID
  • Doxycycline 100 mg daily/BID
  • Biologic therapy under specialist care when severe
 Biologics usually require PA, severity staging, failed antibiotics, specialist documentation. Topicals/oral antibiotics low to moderate; biologics very expensive without coverage. Partial
Photos helpful, but in-person exam often needed.		 Refer
Abscess, sinus tracts, recurrent drainage, severe pain, systemic symptoms.

Acne Visit Documentation Checklist

History
  • Onset, duration, progression
  • Location: face, chest, back, shoulders
  • Lesion type: comedones, papules, pustules, nodules, cysts
  • Pain, itching, scarring, post-inflammatory hyperpigmentation
  • Triggers: menses, stress, sweating, mask/helmet, cosmetics, hair oils
Prior Treatment / Insurance Support
  • OTC benzoyl peroxide, salicylic acid, adapalene
  • Prescription failures and duration used
  • Document failed OTC treatment for 8+ weeks when accurate
  • Severity: mild, moderate, severe
  • Presence of scarring or nodules
Safety Screening
  • Pregnancy or trying to conceive
  • Breastfeeding
  • Medication allergies
  • Kidney/liver disease when relevant
  • Current medications and interaction risk

Telehealth Photo Requirements

  • Front face photo in good lighting.
  • Right and left side facial photos.
  • Close-up of affected area.
  • Chest/back photos if involved.
  • Ask patient to avoid heavy makeup/filtering for diagnostic photos.

Common Follow-Up Plan

For uncomplicated mild to moderate acne, follow up in 6-8 weeks with updated photos. Counsel that retinoids and acne regimens often require 8-12 weeks for visible improvement. Review irritation, dryness, pregnancy status, adherence, and any adverse effects.
